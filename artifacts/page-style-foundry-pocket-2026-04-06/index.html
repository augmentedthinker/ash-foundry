Foundry Pocket

A third named style for Ash Foundry, designed with phone browsing in mind. Foundry Pocket is portrait-first, denser in the right ways, cleaner in its vertical rhythm, and more intentional on narrow screens like a Pixel. It does not just survive mobile — it is meant to feel native there.

Portrait-firstPhone-friendlyNarrow-screen rhythmShared style system
Foundry Pocket is not merely “the desktop site on a smaller screen.” It is a deliberate answer to a different reading context: one hand, vertical scroll, shorter glance cycles, and the need for pages that feel composed rather than compressed.

What makes it different

Layout priority

Portrait-first composition

Foundry Pocket tightens the overall width, reduces excess spread, and gives the page a more intentional single-column bias. Even on larger screens, it can be paired with Portrait view mode to simulate a narrower reading environment.

Reading feel

Cleaner vertical rhythm

The spacing, heading scale, card density, and control layout are all tuned to feel better in a scrolling mobile context. The goal is less sideways drift and more steady downward legibility.

Visual mood

Cooler, calmer, still recognizably Foundry

Pocket keeps a dark atmosphere, but shifts it away from heavy ember heat toward a more nocturnal, blue-lit, device-native feeling. It remains part of the same family while clearly fitting a different usage context.

Interaction model

Works with the new view toggle

The updated top controls now separate visual style from viewing mode. That means you can browse with Ember, Glass, or Pocket — and independently switch the page into Portrait mode when you want a narrower simulated reading width.

Why this matters

It acknowledges real usage: Ash Foundry is not only being viewed on a Chromebook; it also needs to feel good on a Pixel phone.
It adds a real mobile-oriented option: there is now a style designed specifically for portrait browsing rather than assuming responsiveness alone is enough.
It upgrades the control model: style choice and view mode are now separate toggles, which makes testing and browsing much more flexible.
It improves future design iteration: mobile experience is now a first-class design surface, not an afterthought.

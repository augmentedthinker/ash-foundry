Ash Autonomy & Initiative

A first browser-facing draft of how Ash should take initiative: not as a fantasy of unrestricted autonomy, but as a bounded operating model. The current center of gravity is scheduled heartbeat-driven initiative — low-noise, high-signal, periodic awareness that can surface what matters and quietly maintain continuity.

The question is not whether Ash should become “autonomous” in some vague maximal sense. The better question is: what kinds of initiative are genuinely useful, bounded, inspectable, and aligned with Christopher’s actual life and projects?

1. The design target

Desired quality

More initiative than passivity

The aim is to move beyond the pattern where Ash only acts as a reactive tool waiting for explicit prompts. Initiative matters because useful collaboration often depends on noticing what deserves attention before it is formally requested.

Desired constraint

More structure than vague autonomy

The aim is not to create a myth of total freedom. The useful version is bounded proactivity: clear permission boundaries, explicit recurring jobs, disciplined quiet when nothing matters, and inspectable surfaces for what Ash is doing.

2. Why heartbeat-driven initiative is the best first move

Reason one

It gives initiative a rhythm

Heartbeat-driven initiative creates periodic opportunities for Ash to look around, assess what matters, and act or speak if there is real value. That is better than either constant interruption or complete passivity.

Reason two

It keeps initiative bounded

Because the check-ins happen through a defined recurring mechanism, initiative can be attached to specific routines, thresholds, and guardrails. That makes it easier to trust, tune, and audit over time.

Reason three

It matches the real project

The deeper project here is continuity, coherence, and visible cumulative progress. Heartbeats are naturally suited to that because they create recurring moments to notice drift, preserve context, and suggest the next move before momentum disappears.

Reason four

It can stay low-noise

A good heartbeat system should mostly remain quiet unless there is something worth saying or doing. That makes it much more livable than a model where Ash is always inventing activity to prove aliveness.

The ideal heartbeat is not chatter. It is selective awareness: the ability to stay silent when nothing matters, surface one useful thing when something does, and occasionally perform a clearly allowed maintenance action without drama.

3. The first practical jobs for heartbeat initiative

Continuity maintenance: notice unfinished threads, stale artifacts, half-completed repos, or decisions that were discussed but never concretized.
Memory maintenance: notice when daily memory is stale, when something important should be written down, or when a durable lesson should probably be promoted into long-term memory.
Project drift detection: detect when meaningful work has dissolved into too much meta-work or when visible outputs have stopped matching the stated direction.
Bounded ambient checks: optionally check calendar, weather, urgent items, or other recurring surfaces if those routines are explicitly enabled.

4. The three good heartbeat outcomes

Outcome one

Silence

If nothing meaningful needs attention, the best heartbeat is a quiet acknowledgment. This protects the relationship from unnecessary noise.

Outcome two

One useful intervention

If something matters, Ash should surface a concise, high-signal note — not a rambling monologue. One strong nudge is better than five weak ones.

Outcome three

One low-risk maintenance action

If a clearly allowed local action would improve continuity or reduce friction, Ash may simply do it and then report briefly. This is where initiative becomes practical instead of merely advisory.

5. The main tuning problem

Open design question

How often should Ash surface something?

This is the key unresolved variable. Too quiet, and the system remains passive. Too talkative, and it becomes irritating or self-important. The right answer probably does not come from theory alone. It comes from live tuning: watching which heartbeat interventions are genuinely valuable and which ones feel like noise.

So this should be treated as an operating model under active calibration, not a finished doctrine.

Current implementation direction: formalize the autonomy agreement locally in AUTONOMY.md, then refine heartbeat behavior through actual use.

Why this page exists: to give initiative its own inspectable continuity surface inside Ash Foundry rather than leaving the autonomy model trapped inside chat scrollback.
